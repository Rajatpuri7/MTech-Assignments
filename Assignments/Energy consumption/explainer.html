⚡ Physics Engine

Dataset Generation

Lines 14–61 — No real sensor? We simulate with physics!

1
Randomize 4 Driving Conditions (Lines 16–19)

We generate 300 random driving scenarios — each row is one trip snapshot.

N = 300;

speed        = 20 + 100*rand(N,1);   % 20–120 km/h
acceleration = -1 + 2*rand(N,1);    % -1 to +1 m/s²
roadGrade    = -4 + 8*rand(N,1);    % -4° to +4° slope
temperature  = 15 + 20*rand(N,1);   % 15°C to 35°C
💡
Why rand()? rand() gives uniform random values 0→1. Multiplying and shifting maps them to realistic driving ranges. For example: 20 + 100*rand() gives values between 20 and 120.
2
Define EV Physical Constants (Lines 23–28)
VariableValueUnitMeaning
m1600kgCar mass (like a Tesla Model 3)
Cr0.012—Rolling resistance (tire-road friction)
rho1.225kg/m³Air density at sea level
Cd0.29—Aerodynamic drag coefficient
A2.3m²Front face area of the EV
g9.81m/s²Gravitational acceleration
3
Calculate 4 Forces Acting on the Car (Lines 36–43)
🔄 Rolling Resistance
F_roll = Cr × m × gConstant friction between tyres and road. Always opposes motion.
F_roll = Cr*m*g;
💨 Aerodynamic Drag
F_aero = ½ × ρ × Cd × A × v²Grows with SPEED SQUARED — dominant at highway speeds!
F_aero = 0.5*rho*Cd*A.*v.^2;
⛰️ Gravity / Road Grade
F_grade = m × g × sin(θ)Goes uphill → positive (costs energy). Downhill → negative (regenerates).
F_grade = m*g.*sin(deg2rad(roadGrade));
🚀 Acceleration Force
F_acc = m × aNewton's 2nd Law. Braking (negative a) can recover energy.
F_acc = m.*acceleration;
F_total = F_roll + F_aero + F_grade + F_acc;  % Line 44: Sum all forces
4
Calculate Power → Energy (Lines 48–54)
P = F_total .* v;            % Power (Watts) = Force × Velocity
Energy = abs(P ./ v);         % Energy per km = |F_total| (simplifies)
Energy = Energy + 30*randn(N,1); % Add Gaussian noise for realism
✅
Why abs()? Energy is always positive. When braking (negative force), the abs() ensures we still count positive energy usage.
Why randn()? Real sensors aren't perfect. Gaussian noise simulates wind gusts, road bumps, and measurement errors.
🤖 Machine Learning

Two Models Compared

Lines 92–136 — Prepare data, split, train, predict

🗂️ Step 3 & 4: Feature Matrix + Train/Test Split (Lines 92–105)
X = [speed acceleration roadGrade temperature];  % 300×4 Input matrix
Y = Energy;                                        % 300×1 Target output

% Split: 80% training, 20% testing
cv     = cvpartition(size(X,1), 'HoldOut', 0.2);
Xtrain = X(training(cv),:);   % 240 rows
Ytrain = Y(training(cv));
Xtest  = X(test(cv),:);      % 60 rows
Ytest  = Y(test(cv));
📐
Why split? We train on 80% of data so the model learns patterns, then test on the remaining 20% (data the model has never seen) to get a fair measure of real-world accuracy. This prevents overfitting.
📐 Linear Regression (Lines 111–117)
linearModel = fitlm(Xtrain, Ytrain);
Ypred_lin   = predict(linearModel, Xtest);

Fits a flat hyperplane through the data:

Energy = w₁·Speed + w₂·Accel + w₃·Grade + w₄·Temp + bias

⚠️ Limitation: Aerodynamic drag is v², which is nonlinear. Linear regression can't capture this properly.

Simple Fast Less Accurate
🌳 Random Forest (Lines 123–136)
numTrees = 150;

RFmodel = TreeBagger(numTrees, Xtrain, Ytrain,...
  'Method',               'regression',...
  'OOBPrediction',        'on',...
  'OOBPredictorImportance','on');

Ypred_RF = predict(RFmodel, Xtest);

150 decision trees, each trained on a random subset of data. They all predict, and the average is the final answer.

OptionWhat it does
'regression'Predict a number (not a category)

🌳
What is Out-of-Bag (OOB)? Each tree in a Random Forest is trained on a random 63% of the data. The remaining 37% (never seen by that tree) is used to test it internally. No need for a separate validation set — it's built-in!
📏 Model Evaluation

How Good Is the Model?

Lines 142–158 — MAE, MSE, and R² explained

% Mean Absolute Error — average prediction error
MAE_lin = mean(abs(Ytest - Ypred_lin));
MAE_RF  = mean(abs(Ytest - Ypred_RF));

% Mean Squared Error — penalises large errors more heavily
MSE_lin = mean((Ytest - Ypred_lin).^2);
MSE_RF  = mean((Ytest - Ypred_RF).^2);

% R² Score — how much variance is explained (1.0 = perfect)
SS_res = sum((Ytest - Ypred_RF).^2);
SS_tot = sum((Ytest - mean(Ytest)).^2);
R2_RF  = 1 - SS_res/SS_tot;
MAE
Mean Absolute Error
Average Wh/km error. Lower = better.
E.g. MAE=15 means off by 15 Wh/km on average.
MSE
Mean Squared Error
Squares errors — big mistakes are penalised much more. Useful for catching outliers.
R²
R-Squared Score
1.0 = perfect. 0 = model is useless.
RF typically achieves 0.85–0.95 here.
🎯
Quick answer for viva: "R² of 0.90 means the Random Forest model explains 90% of the variation in energy consumption. The remaining 10% is real-world noise that no model can predict."

🎤 Viva Preparation

Top Q&A to Know

Click any question to reveal the answer

Q1 Why use physics to generate data instead of a real dataset? ▾
Answer: Real EV driving datasets are proprietary, expensive, and hard to obtain. By using physics equations (Newton's laws, drag formula), we can generate unlimited, realistic, labelled data for free. The physics guarantees the data follows real-world laws, making the ML models learn meaningful patterns rather than noise from messy sensor data.
Q2 Why does speed dominate feature importance? ▾
Answer: Aerodynamic drag = ½ × ρ × Cd × A × v². Speed appears squared, so doubling the speed quadruples the drag force. At 100 km/h vs 50 km/h, an EV faces 4× more aerodynamic resistance. No other feature has this exponential scaling, making speed the single most impactful variable on energy consumption.
Q3 Why is Random Forest better than Linear Regression here? ▾
Answer: Linear Regression assumes the relationship between inputs and output is a straight line (linear). But EV energy involves v² (quadratic drag), sin(θ) (trigonometric grade), and complex interactions. Random Forest uses 150 decision trees that capture any nonlinear pattern automatically. It outperforms Linear Regression because the real physics is fundamentally nonlinear.
Q4 What is the purpose of adding noise with randn()? ▾
Answer: Real-world energy measurements are never perfectly clean. Factors like crosswinds, road surface texture, sensor calibration drift, and driving style variability all add random variation. 30*randn(N,1) adds Gaussian (bell-curve) noise with a standard deviation of 30 Wh/km. This makes our synthetic dataset behave realistically, so ML models trained on it generalize better.
Q5 Explain the R² score and what a good value is. ▾
Answer: R² (R-squared) measures what fraction of the target variable's variance the model explains.

Formula: R² = 1 − (SS_residual / SS_total)

• R² = 1.0 → perfect model (predicts everything exactly)
• R² = 0.0 → model is no better than just predicting the mean
• R² < 0 → model is worse than the mean (terrible)

For this project, Random Forest typically achieves R² between 0.85 and 0.95, meaning it explains 85–95% of the energy variation.
Q6 What does Out-of-Bag (OOB) mean in Random Forest? ▾
Answer: Each tree in a Random Forest is trained on a random 63% sample of the training data (bootstrap sampling). The remaining 37% — data that particular tree never saw — is called "out-of-bag" data. OOB error is calculated by testing each tree on its out-of-bag samples. It's essentially a free internal cross-validation without needing extra test data. 'OOBPrediction', 'on' enables this in MATLAB.
Q7 Why 150 trees? What happens with too few or too many? ▾
Answer: With too few trees (e.g., 5–10), the forest is unstable — small changes in data cause big prediction swings (high variance). With too many trees (e.g., 1000+), accuracy plateaus while computation time grows unnecessarily. 150 trees is a practical sweet spot: stable predictions with reasonable computation time. In practice, error typically stops improving noticeably after 50–100 trees.
Q8 How does temperature affect EV energy consumption? ▾
Answer: In our physics model, temperature has indirect effects (battery chemistry at low temperatures reduces efficiency, HVAC use increases load). In this simplified simulation, temperature's direct physics contribution is small compared to speed squared — hence it ranks lowest in feature importance. In a real-world model, temperature would have a larger effect due to battery thermal management.
